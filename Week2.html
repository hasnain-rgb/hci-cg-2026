Thinkers and Paradigms

This section maps major HCI thinkers to theories, rules, heuristics, and frameworks.

Key Thinkers

Card, Moran & Newell (1983) — Theory

Category: Theory / Predictive Models

They proposed a scientific theory of humans as information processors. Their GOMS and KLM models predict user performance time.

Reference: Dix et al., HCI, Chapter 12

Don Norman (1988) — Conceptual Interaction Theory

Category: Theory (Conceptual Interaction Models)

Norman’s model explains why users succeed or fail through gulfs of execution and evaluation.

Reference: Dix et al., HCI, Chapter 9

Ben Shneiderman (1983) — Design Rules

Category: Design Rules

He provided prescriptive principles like consistency, feedback, and error prevention.

Reference: Dix et al., HCI, Chapter 9

Jakob Nielsen (1991) — Usability Heuristics

Category: Heuristics

Nielsen’s heuristics are practical guidelines for finding usability problems.

Reference: Dix et al., HCI, Chapter 9

Dix, Finlay, Abowd & Beale — Interaction Framework

Category: Framework

The Interaction Framework connects user, system, input, output, and context.

Reference: Dix et al., HCI, Chapter 2 & 9

Norman’s 7 Stages of Action

  1. Form Goal – Decide what you want to achieve.
  2. Form Intention – Choose to act to achieve that goal.
  3. Specify Action – Plan the specific action to take.
  4. Execute Action – Perform the action on the system.
  5. Perceive State – Notice what the system shows after the action.
  6. Interpret State – Understand what that feedback means.
  7. Evaluate Outcome – Check whether your goal was achieved.

Reference: Dix et al., HCI, Chapter 9 (Norman’s Action Cycle)

Gulfs of Execution and Evaluation

Gulf of Execution

The gap between what the user wants to do and what actions the system allows.

Example: A user wants to send money but cannot find the “Pay” button.

Gulf of Evaluation

The gap between what the system shows and what the user understands.

Example: The system shows a message, but the user cannot tell if payment was successful.

Reference: Dix et al., HCI, Chapter 9
